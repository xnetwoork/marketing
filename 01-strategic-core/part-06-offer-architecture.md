# Offer Architecture — Core, Upsell & Cross-Sell

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:
- Design a complete offer stack that maximizes customer value and revenue
- Optimize your core offer for conversion and retention
- Build natural upsell and cross-sell mechanics into your product and sales motion
- Apply effective bundling and pricing strategies to increase deal size
- Use psychological principles to make your offer more compelling
- Test and iterate your offer architecture systematically

---

## What Is Offer Architecture?

Offer architecture is the deliberate design of **what you sell, how it's packaged, at what price, and in what sequence** — so that every customer transaction moves customers up a value ladder while deepening their relationship with your company.

Most companies let their offer stack grow organically — a new product here, a discount there, a bundle invented by a desperate sales rep. The result is confusion for buyers and leakage for the business.

A designed offer stack does the opposite: it creates **a clear path of value escalation** that feels natural to the customer and maximizes revenue per customer over time.

---

## The Offer Stack Hierarchy

**Level 1: Core Offer**
The primary product or service. The thing you're known for. Solves the main problem.

**Level 2: Core Enhancers**
Add-ons that make the core offer more effective or easier to adopt (onboarding, training, integrations, premium support).

**Level 3: Upsells**
Higher-tier versions of the core offer — more capacity, more features, more users, more power.

**Level 4: Cross-Sells**
Adjacent products that solve a related but distinct problem for the same customer.

**Level 5: Strategic Expansion**
New platforms, ecosystems, or long-term relationships (enterprise agreements, multi-year contracts, channel partnerships).

---

## Core Offer Optimization

The core offer must accomplish three things:
1. **Solve a specific problem** for a specific customer at a specific moment
2. **Deliver clear, measurable value** fast enough to justify renewal
3. **Create the context and appetite** for upsells and cross-sells

**Core offer optimization checklist:**

| Dimension | Key question | Optimization lever |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Does the buyer immediately understand what they get? | Simplify packaging; rename to outcome (not feature) |
| **Time-to-value** | How long until the customer gets the first "aha"? | Reduce onboarding friction; add activation milestones |
| **Proof of value** | Can the customer quantify what they've gained? | Build in-product ROI dashboards or usage reports |
| **Renewal trigger** | Is there a natural reason to continue and expand? | Tie value to ongoing use, not one-time setup |

## Upsell Mechanics

An upsell is a revenue expansion with an existing customer — moving them to a higher tier, larger volume, or premium version. The best upsells feel like a natural next step, not a sales pitch.

### Upsell Models

**1. Seat/User Expansion**
*As teams grow, they pay more.*
- Trigger: Team size grows; sharing logins; waitlisted users
- Signal: Usage by non-licensed users; team growth in CRM
- Classic example: Slack, Zoom, Notion

**2. Capacity Expansion**
*As usage grows, they pay more.*
- Trigger: Approaching limits (API calls, data storage, contacts, sends)
- Signal: Usage metrics approaching threshold
- Classic example: AWS, Twilio, Snowflake

**3. Feature Tier Upgrade**
*Unlock advanced capabilities for power users.*
- Trigger: Customer tries to use locked feature; CS discovers unmet need
- Signal: Feature page views; trial of gated features; support tickets requesting capability
- Classic example: HubSpot (Starter → Professional → Enterprise)

**4. Outcome-Based Upsell**
*Customer has achieved success; now they want more.*
- Trigger: Customer reaches a milestone and wants to scale the result
- Signal: High NPS, high feature adoption, growing engagement
- Best practice: Build upsell conversation into QBRs and success check-ins

### Upsell Best Practices
- **Time upsell conversations to momentum**, not calendar — reach out when usage spikes, not on a fixed quarterly schedule
- **Let product trigger the conversation** — in-app prompts at the right moment outperform cold CS outreach
- **Make the upgrade feel earned** — customers who are succeeding with the core offer are psychologically ready to expand

## Cross-Sell Sequencing

Cross-sells are products that solve a related but different problem for the same customer. The key is **sequencing** — selling the right product at the right stage of the customer lifecycle.

### Cross-Sell Sequence Map

| Customer Stage | Core Use Case | Ideal Cross-Sell |
|---|---|---|
| **Activation** (0–30 days) | Learning core product | Onboarding/training add-on |
| **Adoption** (30–90 days) | Integrating into workflow | Integrations, connectors, or APIs |
| **Value Realization** (90–180 days) | Seeing results from core product | Analytics or reporting add-on |
| **Expansion** (180+ days) | Scaling use case | Adjacent product for second pain point |
| **Advocate** (12+ months) | Reference customer | Strategic partnership, multi-product contract |

> **Rule:** Don't cross-sell before the customer has realized value from the core offer. Cross-selling to a struggling customer accelerates churn, not revenue.

## Bundling Strategies

Bundles increase average order value, reduce price friction, and create switching costs.

**Four bundling approaches:**

1. **Pure Bundle** — products only sold together (all-or-nothing)
   - Pros: Simplicity; high perceived value
   - Cons: Can feel forced; limits flexibility
   - When to use: When products are highly complementary and rarely bought separately

2. **Mixed Bundle** — products available individually or together, with a bundle discount
   - Pros: Appeals to both segments; discount drives bundle choice
   - Cons: Requires clear pricing; cannibalization risk
   - When to use: Standard for SaaS tiers (Starter/Pro/Enterprise)

3. **Cross-Product Bundle** — pairs core offer with a cross-sell at a discount
   - Pros: Drives cross-sell adoption; increases LTV
   - Cons: Requires education; can confuse buyers
   - When to use: Post-adoption; CS-led expansion

4. **Solution Bundle** — all products + services + onboarding + support in one price
   - Pros: Enterprise-friendly; eliminates procurement friction
   - Cons: Pricing complexity; may cannibalize services revenue
   - When to use: Enterprise accounts with high implementation needs

## Value-Based Pricing in Offers

Each offer tier and add-on should be priced relative to the **value delivered to the customer**, not your cost to deliver it.

**Value density framework:**
- What is the measurable outcome this offer enables? (Revenue gained, cost saved, time freed)
- What would it cost the customer to achieve the same outcome without your product?
- What percentage of that value is it reasonable to capture as price?

**Typical software value capture rates:**
- 10–20% of quantifiable customer value = defensible price
- Under 5% = likely under-priced; test higher
- Over 30% = risk of churn when customer calculates ROI

**Apply per offer component:**
- Core offer: Price to the primary outcome
- Upsell: Price to the incremental outcome (not the full value stack)
- Cross-sell: Price to the new problem solved — independent of core pricing

## Psychological Principles in Offer Design

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | Show the highest-priced tier first; it makes the middle tier feel reasonable |
| **Decoy effect** | Add a third option that makes your preferred option look like the obvious choice |
| **Loss aversion** | Frame the offer around what the customer loses by not upgrading |
| **Social proof** | Show which tier is "most popular" — buyers default to what others choose |
| **Scarcity/urgency** | Time-limited pricing or feature availability triggers action |
| **Chunking** | Break complex offers into digestible packages; don't overwhelm with options |

## Offer Testing

Never finalize an offer architecture without testing it. Test systematically:

1. **Price sensitivity test** — Van Westendorp pricing survey: "At what price is this too cheap? Too expensive? Good value? Acceptable?"
2. **Willingness-to-pay test** — A/B test pricing pages with different anchor prices
3. **Bundle preference test** — Show two different bundles to different segments; measure conversion
4. **Upsell timing test** — Test in-product upsell prompts at Day 14 vs. Day 30 vs. first value milestone
5. **Naming test** — "Professional" vs. "Growth" vs. "Scale" tier names affect conversion; test them

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Offer architecture is a system**, not a product list — design the path customers take through your value stack.
- **Core offer quality determines all expansion potential** — if the core fails, upsells and cross-sells fail too.
- **Upsell timing is as important as upsell offer** — momentum-triggered upsells outperform calendar-triggered ones.
- **Cross-sell sequence matters** — sell to the next problem *after* the current one is solved.
- **Pricing is part of the offer** — use value-based pricing and psychological principles deliberately.
- **Test every offer assumption** — pricing, bundling, naming, and timing are all variables.

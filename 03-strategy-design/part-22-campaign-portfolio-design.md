# Part 22: Campaign Portfolio Design — Always-On & Burst Campaigns

## Learning Objectives
- Design a balanced campaign portfolio across the full funnel
- Understand the role and mechanics of always-on vs burst campaigns
- Balance brand and performance investment for sustainable growth
- Build a campaign brief framework and resource planning model

---

## The Campaign Portfolio Concept

A **campaign portfolio** treats marketing programmes as a managed collection of investments — each with a defined purpose, risk profile, time horizon, and expected return. Just as a financial portfolio balances growth assets and stable income assets, a campaign portfolio balances long-term brand building with short-term performance delivery.

**The two primary campaign types:**

| Dimension | Always-On | Burst |
|---|---|---|
| **Duration** | Continuous (12+ months) | Fixed window (days to 8 weeks) |
| **Goal** | Compound growth, brand, SEO, retention | Accelerate specific outcomes in a time window |
| **Targeting** | Broad reach with intent filters | Precise audience, specific offer |
| **Budget model** | Fixed monthly allocation | Project-based; peaks during campaign window |
| **Creative** | Evergreen assets, regularly refreshed | Campaign-specific, high-investment creative |
| **Measurement** | Rolling metrics (ROAS, CAC, brand lift) | Campaign KPIs vs benchmark |

## Always-On Marketing Programmes

Always-on programmes are the engine of compounding growth. They run continuously, improve incrementally, and generate a baseline of leads, traffic, and brand equity that burst campaigns amplify.

### Core Always-On Programmes

**1. Paid Search (Intent Capture)**
- Bid on high-intent keywords your buyers use when they're ready to purchase
- Always-on search captures demand; don't pause it during burst campaigns
- Optimise weekly: negative keywords, bid adjustments, quality score improvements

**2. SEO and Organic Content**
- Build content that ranks and compounds over time
- New organic traffic from content published 6 months ago costs nothing marginal
- Target bottom-of-funnel intent terms first, then mid-funnel educational content

**3. Lifecycle Email and Nurture**
- Automated sequences triggered by behaviour (signup, trial start, product usage signals)
- Always on for every customer cohort, every day
- ROI is typically the highest of any marketing channel — underinvested by most companies

**4. Retargeting**
- Re-engage high-intent visitors who didn't convert
- Segment by funnel stage and recency for relevance
- Low-cost always-on programme that extracts more value from existing traffic

**5. Referral and Advocacy Programme**
- Continuously incentivise existing customers to refer new ones
- Compounding: each referred customer can become a referrer
- Requires product-level integration and CS team enablement

## Burst Campaign Strategy

Burst campaigns create **concentrated moments of market attention** that achieve outcomes impossible through slow-burn investment alone.

### When to Deploy a Burst Campaign

- **Product launch** — build awareness rapidly for a new feature or product
- **Seasonal demand** — align with peak buying intent (Black Friday, fiscal year-end, industry events)
- **Competitive response** — counter a competitor's aggressive campaign
- **Category creation** — invest heavily to own a new narrative before competitors do
- **Geographic launch** — rapid awareness building in a new market

### Burst Campaign Mechanics

**1. Duration:** Most burst campaigns peak in 2–4 weeks. Beyond 6–8 weeks, the "burst" benefit diminishes and it becomes always-on spend with burst pricing.

**2. Budget concentration:** Burst budget should represent 1.5–3x your normal weekly spend in the target channel during the campaign window.

**3. Sequencing:** Burst campaigns work best when preceded by always-on warm-up content and followed by nurture sequences to capture burst-generated demand.

**4. Creative commitment:** Burst campaigns require dedicated, high-quality creative investment. Repurposing always-on assets into burst windows usually underperforms.

**Example — SaaS product launch burst:**
- Week -2: Teaser content on LinkedIn and email to existing customers
- Week 0–2 (burst): Paid social + PR + email launch sequence + partner activation
- Week 3–4 (sustain): Retargeting + sales follow-up sequences + content amplification
- Week 5+: New feature folded into always-on messaging

## Portfolio Diversification Across the Funnel

A healthy campaign portfolio invests across all funnel stages. Over-investment in any single stage creates bottlenecks.

### Funnel Stage Portfolio Model

| Funnel Stage | Programme Types | Budget % (Typical) |
|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Brand advertising, PR, content, thought leadership, social | 15–25% |
| **Consideration** | SEO, webinars, comparison content, review site investment | 20–30% |
| **Intent/Decision** | Paid search, retargeting, demo/trial offers, competitive displacement | 25–35% |
| **Conversion/Activation** | Landing page optimisation, onboarding, lifecycle email | 10–15% |
| **Retention/Advocacy** | CS programmes, referral, community, loyalty | 10–20% |

**Diagnosis:** If your portfolio is heavily weighted toward intent/decision (bottom of funnel), you are likely experiencing rising CAC due to insufficient pipeline being created upstream.

## Balancing Brand and Performance Investment

One of the most persistent and consequential debates in marketing. The evidence is clear.

### The Case for Brand Investment

- **Les Binet and Peter Field** research (IPA Effectiveness Databank): Optimal brand-to-performance split is approximately **60% brand / 40% performance** for long-term growth
- Brand investment reduces price sensitivity, improves conversion rates, and lowers future CAC
- Brand effects accrue over 6–18 months — performance effects are immediate but don't compound

### Practical Application by Stage

| Company Stage | Recommended Brand % | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-PMF | 10–15% | Don't invest in brand before product is validated |
| Early growth (<$5M ARR) | 15–25% | Build category awareness as you scale acquisition |
| Growth stage ($5–50M ARR) | 25–40% | Brand investment begins to compound meaningfully |
| Scale ($50M+ ARR) | 40–60% | Brand is now a moat — invest to maintain and extend it |

**The performance trap:** Companies that invest only in performance marketing often see rising CAC as they exhaust in-market demand. Brand investment replenishes that demand pool over time.

## Campaign Sequencing

The order in which campaigns run matters. Poorly sequenced campaigns waste budget by running before their prerequisites are in place.

### Sequencing Principles

1. **Awareness before consideration** — don't run comparison campaigns to buyers who don't know you exist
2. **Trust before conversion** — ensure case studies and social proof are live before running bottom-of-funnel conversion campaigns
3. **Onboarding before expansion** — ensure new customers are successfully activated before promoting upsell
4. **Warm before burst** — prime audiences with always-on content before activating burst spend

### Sample Annual Campaign Sequence (SaaS)

| Quarter | Always-On Focus | Burst Campaign |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | SEO content, lifecycle nurture refresh | New year positioning campaign |
| Q2 | Paid search expansion, retargeting optimise | Spring product launch burst |
| Q3 | Referral programme activation, review site push | Industry conference amplification |
| Q4 | Renewal campaign for annual subscribers | Year-end urgency campaign |

## Campaign Brief Framework

Every campaign — burst or always-on launch — requires a brief. A brief prevents expensive misalignment between strategy and execution.

### Campaign Brief Template

```
CAMPAIGN NAME:
CAMPAIGN TYPE: Always-On / Burst
STRATEGIC OBJECTIVE: [Link to revenue tree node]
CAMPAIGN GOAL: [Specific metric and target]
TARGET AUDIENCE: [Segment, stage, persona]
CORE MESSAGE: [One sentence — what do we want them to believe/feel/do?]
OFFER: [What are we giving them / asking them to do?]
CHANNELS: [Where will this run?]
BUDGET: [Total + breakdown by channel]
TIMELINE: [Start / Peak / Wind-down / End dates]
SUCCESS METRICS: [Primary + secondary KPIs]
CAMPAIGN OWNER: [Named individual]
REVIEW DATE: [Mid-campaign check-in]
```

## Campaign Performance Benchmarks

Use these as starting points — benchmarks vary by industry, channel maturity, and audience.

| Channel | Benchmark Metric | Good Performance |
|---|---|---|
| Paid Search (B2B) | CVR (click to trial/demo) | 3–8% |
| Paid Social (B2B) | CTR | 0.5–1.5% |
| Email (nurture sequence) | Open rate | 25–40% |
| Email (cold outbound) | Reply rate | 3–8% |
| Content / SEO | Organic sessions growth MoM | 5–15% |
| Referral programme | Referral share of new signups | 15–30% |
| Webinar | Attendance-to-pipeline % | 5–15% |

## Key Takeaways

- Always-on programmes build the compounding base; burst campaigns create accelerating moments
- Portfolio diversification across funnel stages prevents CAC-inflating bottlenecks
- Brand and performance investment must be balanced — performance-only strategies hit a ceiling
- Campaign sequencing matters — warm audiences before bursting, and prime trust before converting
- Every campaign needs a brief with a named owner and clear success metric

---

## Actionable Next Steps

1. **Audit your current campaign mix** — categorise each programme as always-on or burst, and map to funnel stage
2. **Identify your most underinvested funnel stage** — this is your biggest portfolio gap
3. **Calculate your brand-to-performance ratio** — is it appropriate for your stage?
4. **Write a campaign brief** for your next burst campaign before any creative is produced
5. **Set up a campaign performance dashboard** with benchmarks visible to the whole team

# Part 19: Copywriting Fundamentals

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the principles of persuasive copywriting that drives action
- Learn the key frameworks and formulas used by professional copywriters
- Apply copywriting principles to improve conversion across all marketing channels

---

## What Is Copywriting?

Copywriting is the craft of writing words that motivate readers to take a specific action — subscribe, download, buy, register, share, or simply continue reading.

It's different from other writing disciplines. Journalism informs. Literature entertains. Academic writing analyzes. **Copywriting persuades.**

Great copywriting feels natural and effortless to read while carefully engineering every element to move the reader toward a specific outcome. The best copy doesn't feel like an ad — it feels like a helpful friend talking to you.

---

## The Fundamental Principle: Write for the Reader

The most common copywriting mistake is writing about yourself — your company's achievements, your product's features, how long you've been in business.

Readers don't care about you. They care about themselves, their problems, and their desired outcomes. The first shift every copywriter must make: stop writing *about* the product and start writing *to the person*.

**The "You" vs. "We" test:** Count how many times your copy says "we," "our," or the company name versus "you" and "your." The second group should dominate.

**Weak:** "Our platform leverages AI to streamline your workflows with cutting-edge automation technology."

**Strong:** "You'll spend 4 fewer hours per week on reports — and those hours are yours back."

## Copywriting Frameworks

### AIDA: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
The oldest and most durable copywriting formula:

1. **Attention:** Hook the reader immediately. If they don't read the first line, nothing else matters.
2. **Interest:** Develop the hook with relevant detail that keeps them reading.
3. **Desire:** Create an emotional want — show what life looks like after the solution.
4. **Action:** Tell them exactly what to do next; make it easy and obvious.

**Application:** Works for ads, emails, landing pages, and social posts.

### PAS: Problem, Agitate, Solution
Particularly powerful for direct response:

1. **Problem:** Name the problem they're experiencing
2. **Agitate:** Deepen the pain — make the problem feel more real, costly, or frustrating
3. **Solution:** Present the relief

**Example:**
- *Problem:* "Your website looks like it was built in 2009."
- *Agitate:* "Every day, potential customers land on it, cringe, and go to a competitor that looks trustworthy."
- *Solution:* "Squarespace gives you a professional website in under an hour — no designer needed."

### BAB: Before, After, Bridge
Focus on transformation:

1. **Before:** Paint the current painful reality
2. **After:** Paint the desired future state
3. **Bridge:** Show how to get from before to after (your product)

**Example:**
- *Before:* "Buried in email, losing track of every conversation."
- *After:* "Inbox zero, every reply handled, focus restored."
- *Bridge:* "Here's how Superhuman users achieve it."

### FAB: Features, Advantages, Benefits
Translate features into benefits:
- **Feature:** What the product does or has
- **Advantage:** Why that feature is better
- **Benefit:** What it means for the customer's life

**Example:** "Automatically syncs across all devices [feature], so your notes are always up-to-date wherever you are [advantage], meaning you can pick up right where you left off — even mid-thought [benefit]."

## The Power of Headlines

In most contexts, the headline is 80% of the copy's power. If the headline doesn't work, nothing else gets read.

David Ogilvy: "On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy."

**What makes a headline work:**
- **Specificity:** "How to Write 1,000 Words in an Hour" > "How to Write Faster"
- **Benefit:** What will the reader get or achieve?
- **Curiosity:** Open a loop that the body copy closes
- **Relevance:** Speaks directly to the reader's situation

**Headline formulas:**
- "How to [achieve desired outcome] without [feared cost]"
- "The [unusual/surprising] way to [achieve outcome]"
- "[Number] [things] every [target audience] should know about [topic]"
- "Why [conventional wisdom] is wrong — and what to do instead"

## Writing That Converts

### Use Conversational Language
Write as you speak. Short sentences. Simple words. Read your copy aloud — if it sounds stiff or formal, rewrite it.

### Be Specific
Vague claims are ignored. Specific claims are believed and remembered.
- Vague: "Our customers see significant results"
- Specific: "Our customers reduce churn by an average of 23% within 90 days"

### Reduce Friction
Make every step of reading easier:
- Use subheadings to break up walls of text
- Use bullet points for lists of benefits or features
- Keep paragraphs short (2-3 sentences max for digital)
- Use white space liberally

### Use Social Proof
Other people's experiences are more credible than your claims:
- Customer testimonials with names and specifics
- Numbers ("10,000 teams use us")
- Logos of recognizable customers
- Awards, certifications, press mentions

### Clear Call to Action
Tell people exactly what to do, make it easy, and remove uncertainty:
- Specific: "Start your free 14-day trial" > "Sign up"
- Benefit-framed: "Get my free guide" > "Download"
- Low-friction: "No credit card required" removes a common hesitation

---

## Copywriting for Different Channels

**Email subject lines:** 6-10 words, curiosity or value; personalization where genuine
**Ad headlines:** Ultra-concise, high relevance to the audience being targeted
**Landing page headlines:** Clear primary benefit, supported by subheadline with detail
**Social captions:** Lead with the hook; the first line must stop the scroll
**Sales emails:** 100-200 words max; one clear objective; one clear CTA

---

## Key Takeaways

- Great copywriting writes to the reader's problems and desires, not about the product
- The headline does 80% of the work; it must earn the reader's continued attention
- Use proven frameworks (AIDA, PAS, BAB) rather than reinventing persuasion from scratch
- Specificity creates credibility; vague claims are ignored or disbelieved
- Every piece of copy should have one clear call to action — multiple CTAs dilute conversion

# Part 20: Brand Voice & Tone

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the difference between brand voice and tone, and why both matter
- Learn how to define a distinctive brand voice that resonates with your audience
- Create guidelines that ensure consistency across all content and communications

---

## Voice vs. Tone: The Key Distinction

**Brand voice** is your consistent personality — who you are as a brand. It doesn't change. It's expressed whether you're writing a blog post, a support email, a tweet, or a legal notice.

**Brand tone** is how you adjust that personality to fit the context. The same person speaks differently at a job interview versus at a backyard barbecue. Your brand voice stays consistent; your tone adapts.

**Analogy:** A person's character (voice) is consistent, but their manner (tone) shifts between serious and playful depending on whether they're consoling a friend or celebrating a promotion.

---

## Why Brand Voice Matters

### Differentiation
In markets crowded with functionally similar products, voice is one of the few remaining differentiators. Mailchimp, Wendy's, and Dollar Shave Club all built significant brand value primarily through distinctive voice.

### Trust and Familiarity
Consistent voice across touchpoints creates familiarity, and familiarity builds trust. When every email sounds like the same person wrote it, customers develop a sense of relationship with the brand.

### Efficiency
A documented voice guide enables every writer — employee, agency, freelancer — to create on-brand content faster, without constant back-and-forth revision.

### Audience Resonance
A well-defined voice attracts the right audience and repels the wrong one. That's not a failure — it's the point.

## The Four Dimensions of Voice

A practical voice definition describes four dimensions:

### 1. Character
What personality archetype best describes your brand?
- The Expert: authoritative, knowledgeable, precise
- The Rebel: unconventional, provocative, against the grain
- The Friend: warm, approachable, helpful
- The Innovator: forward-thinking, exciting, visionary
- The Mentor: wise, supportive, empowering

Choose a primary archetype and 1-2 secondary characteristics.

### 2. Language
- **Vocabulary level:** Technical/sophisticated or simple/accessible?
- **Sentence length:** Short and punchy or longer and flowing?
- **Jargon:** Industry terminology embraced or avoided?
- **Contractions:** "You'll" or "You will"?

### 3. Perspective
- Do you use "we" (corporate first person), "I" (singular personal), or "you" (audience-centered)?
- Do you take strong positions or remain neutral?
- Is humor a tool you use, and if so, what kind (self-deprecating, witty, absurdist)?

### 4. Values
What principles underlie how you communicate?
- Honesty over flattery?
- Simplicity over comprehensiveness?
- Warmth over professionalism?
- Substance over style?

## Defining Your Voice: A Process

### Step 1: Audit Existing Content
If you have existing content, audit it:
- Which pieces best represent the brand at its best?
- What words and phrases recur?
- Where does the voice feel authentic vs. forced?

### Step 2: Anchor in Your Audience
Your voice must resonate with your target audience, not just reflect your internal preferences. What tone does your ideal customer use in their own communications? What would feel natural to them?

A B2B enterprise security company and a consumer fintech app serving Gen Z will need very different voices — even if they share the same core values of honesty and clarity.

### Step 3: Use the "We Are / We Are Not" Framework
Define your voice by stating what you are AND what you're not. The contrast sharpens the definition.

**Example (project management tool for creatives):**
- We are: Direct, not blunt
- We are: Warm, not corporate
- We are: Confident, not arrogant
- We are: Helpful, not preachy
- We are: Conversational, not casual

### Step 4: Provide Examples
Abstract adjectives ("warm," "authentic," "confident") mean different things to different writers. The voice guide must include examples:

**Topic: Acknowledging a mistake**
- **Sounds like us:** "We got this wrong. Here's what happened and exactly what we're doing to fix it."
- **Doesn't sound like us:** "We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused and assure you that we are actively addressing this matter."

## Tone in Practice: Adapting Context

Your voice is consistent; your tone adapts:

| Situation | Tone Adjustment |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Celebrating a product launch | Energized, celebratory |
| Explaining a complex feature | Clear, thorough, patient |
| Responding to a complaint | Empathetic, direct, solutions-focused |
| Social media banter | Playful, spontaneous |
| Legal/terms of service | Precise, factual (but still readable) |
| Marketing campaign | Engaging, persuasive |

The mistake brands make is applying the same tone to every situation — being playful in a service outage message, or being stiff and formal in casual social content.

---

## The Brand Voice Guide

Document your voice in a guide that includes:

1. **Voice overview:** 3-5 adjectives that describe your brand, with explanation
2. **What you are / what you're not:** 5-8 contrast pairs
3. **Tone variations by context:** How to adjust in different situations
4. **Writing principles:** Rules of style (Oxford comma? Numerals vs. spelled-out numbers? Exclamation point policy?)
5. **Word list:** Words and phrases to embrace and avoid
6. **Before/after examples:** Rewrites that demonstrate the voice in action

Make it accessible and shareable. The best voice guides are short enough to actually be used.

---

## Evolving Your Voice

Brand voice should evolve gradually, not dramatically. A voice that shifts dramatically confuses existing customers and dilutes the brand equity you've built.

When to revisit:
- Significant company evolution (new target market, major pivot)
- Voice consistently underperforming with your target audience
- Acquisition or merger that requires brand integration
- Annual review as part of brand strategy

---

## Key Takeaways

- Voice is consistent personality; tone adapts to context — understand the difference
- A distinctive voice differentiates your brand, builds trust, and improves content efficiency
- Define voice through archetype, language style, perspective, and values
- Document the voice with concrete examples, not just abstract adjectives
- The "We Are / We Are Not" contrast framework sharpens definitions meaningfully

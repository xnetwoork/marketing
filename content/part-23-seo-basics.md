# Part 23: SEO Basics for Marketers

## Learning Objectives
- Understand how search engines work and why organic search matters
- Learn the foundational elements of on-page and off-page SEO
- Build an SEO strategy that drives consistent, qualified traffic to your content

---

## Why SEO Is a Marketing Foundation

Search engine optimization is the process of improving your website and content to rank higher in search engine results pages (SERPs) for queries your target audience makes.

Why it matters:
- **Scale:** Google processes 8.5 billion searches per day. The market for attention on search is enormous.
- **Intent:** Search traffic is high-intent — people searching for solutions are often ready to act
- **Compounding returns:** A well-optimized page ranks and generates traffic for years without ongoing ad spend
- **Cost efficiency:** Over time, organic traffic has a fraction of the cost-per-visitor of paid channels

The downside: SEO is not fast. Results typically compound over 6-18 months. This is precisely why starting now matters.

## How Search Engines Work

Understanding SEO requires a basic model of how search engines operate:

### Crawling
Search engine bots ("crawlers" or "spiders") systematically browse the web, discovering pages by following links. If your pages aren't linked from anywhere, they may not be found.

### Indexing
After crawling, the search engine analyzes each page and stores it in an index — a massive database of all web content. Content is analyzed for topic, quality, and relevance.

### Ranking
When a user makes a query, the search engine evaluates hundreds of factors to determine which pages best answer the query. The ranking algorithm weights signals including:
- **Relevance:** Does the page content match the query's intent?
- **Authority:** Do other high-quality sites link to this page?
- **Quality:** Is the content comprehensive, accurate, and helpful?
- **Technical performance:** Page speed, mobile-friendliness, HTTPS security
- **User experience:** Do searchers click the result and stay engaged?

## Keyword Research: The Foundation

Effective SEO begins with understanding what your audience searches for.

### The Three Dimensions of Keywords

**Search Volume:** How many times is this term searched per month? High volume = more opportunity (and usually more competition).

**Keyword Difficulty:** How hard is it to rank? Determined by the authority and optimization of pages currently ranking. New sites should target lower-difficulty keywords.

**Search Intent:** What is the searcher trying to accomplish?
- **Informational:** "How to write a marketing plan" (researching)
- **Navigational:** "HubSpot login" (finding a specific site)
- **Commercial:** "Best project management software" (comparing options)
- **Transactional:** "Buy Asana Pro plan" (ready to act)

Match your content type to the intent. Trying to rank a product page for an informational query will fail.

### Keyword Research Tools
- **Google Search Console** (free): Shows what you already rank for
- **Google Keyword Planner** (free): Volume and competition data
- **SEMrush / Ahrefs** (paid): Comprehensive keyword and competitive data
- **AnswerThePublic** (freemium): Question-based keyword discovery

### Building a Keyword List
1. Seed keywords: core terms describing your product category
2. Expand with synonyms, variants, and long-tail phrases
3. Research competitor keywords (what do they rank for that you don't?)
4. Prioritize by: search volume × intent match × feasibility (inverse of difficulty)

## On-Page SEO

On-page SEO optimizes individual pages to rank for target keywords.

### Title Tag
The HTML `<title>` element — what appears as the blue link in search results. Include primary keyword near the beginning; keep under 60 characters.

### Meta Description
The snippet below the title in search results. Doesn't directly affect ranking, but affects click-through rate. Write as a mini ad: include keywords and a compelling reason to click; keep under 160 characters.

### Headings (H1, H2, H3)
- One H1 per page — the main headline, containing the primary keyword
- H2s for major sections — use secondary and related keywords naturally
- H3s for subsections

### Content Quality
- **Comprehensive:** Cover the topic thoroughly; answer every reasonable question a searcher might have
- **Unique:** Add value beyond what's already ranking (original data, deeper examples, better structure)
- **Readable:** Short paragraphs, clear headings, scannable formatting
- **Updated:** Refresh content regularly; recency is a ranking signal for many topics

### URL Structure
Use short, descriptive, keyword-containing URLs: `/blog/email-marketing-guide` not `/blog/p=2847`

### Internal Linking
Link to related content within your site. This passes "authority" between pages and helps search engines understand your site structure.

### Images
- Use descriptive file names: `email-open-rate-chart.png` not `IMG_4832.png`
- Write alt text that describes the image (also improves accessibility)
- Compress images for faster load times

## Off-Page SEO: Building Authority

A page's authority is largely determined by who links to it. Backlinks from credible, relevant sites are the most important ranking factor outside your own site.

### Link Building Strategies

**Create linkworthy content:**
- Original research and data (journalists and bloggers cite original data)
- Comprehensive guides that become reference resources
- Free tools, templates, and calculators

**Earn press and PR coverage:**
- Media coverage produces high-authority backlinks
- Publish data or perspectives that are newsworthy in your industry

**Guest posting:**
- Contribute content to relevant industry sites with a backlink to your site

**Digital PR:**
- Pitch journalists with original stories, data, or expert perspectives

### What to Avoid
- Buying links (violates Google guidelines; can result in penalties)
- Low-quality directory submissions
- Link farms or reciprocal linking schemes

---

## Technical SEO

Technical factors affect whether search engines can crawl, index, and rank your content:

- **Page speed:** Use Google PageSpeed Insights; compress images, enable caching
- **Mobile-friendliness:** Google ranks mobile-first; test all key pages on mobile
- **HTTPS:** SSL certificate is a basic ranking signal and trust indicator
- **Core Web Vitals:** Google's UX metrics (LCP, FID, CLS) — optimize for real user experience
- **Sitemap:** XML sitemap helps search engines discover all your pages
- **Robots.txt:** Controls which pages crawlers can and cannot access

---

## Key Takeaways

- SEO drives compounding, high-intent organic traffic that becomes more cost-efficient over time
- Keyword research must consider volume, difficulty, and search intent
- On-page optimization (titles, headings, content quality) makes pages relevant; off-page optimization (backlinks) makes pages authoritative
- Technical SEO ensures search engines can properly crawl and index your content
- SEO results compound over months and years; start now, measure consistently

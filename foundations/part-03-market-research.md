# Part 3: Market Research

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the types and methods of market research
- Learn how to conduct efficient, actionable research on a limited budget
- Turn research findings into strategic insights that drive decisions

---

## Why Market Research Is Non-Negotiable

Every business decision rests on assumptions. Market research is the discipline of testing and validating those assumptions before committing significant resources. Without it, you're flying blind — spending marketing budget on messages that don't resonate, channels your audience doesn't use, and products solving problems nobody has.

The cost of skipping research is high: CB Insights found that 42% of failed startups cited "no market need" as the primary reason for failure. That's not a product failure — it's a research failure.

But research doesn't need to be expensive or complex. Even informal conversations with ten potential customers can reveal insights that transform your strategy.

---

## Types of Market Research

### Primary Research
You collect the data yourself, directly from your sources. It's more expensive and time-consuming, but it gives you unique insights your competitors don't have.

**Qualitative methods** — explore the "why" behind behavior:
- **In-depth interviews** — 30-60 minute conversations with customers or prospects
- **Focus groups** — group discussions moderated to surface opinions and reactions
- **Ethnographic research** — observing customers in their natural environment
- **Usability testing** — watching people use your product or website

**Quantitative methods** — measure the "what" and "how many":
- **Surveys** — structured questionnaires distributed to a defined sample
- **Experiments** — A/B tests, pricing tests, feature flags
- **Analytics** — behavioral data from your website, app, or product

### Secondary Research
You use data already collected by others. Faster and cheaper, but less specific to your unique situation.

- **Industry reports** — Gartner, Nielsen, Forrester, Statista, IBISWorld
- **Government data** — census data, economic statistics, trade data
- **Academic studies** — Google Scholar, university research
- **Competitor analysis** — public filings, website analysis, media coverage
- **Social listening** — online conversations in forums, reviews, social platforms

## The Market Research Process

### Step 1: Define the Research Question

Great research starts with a clear, specific question. Don't start with "We want to understand our market." Start with:
- "Why do customers churn after 90 days?"
- "What factors determine whether a prospect chooses us or Competitor X?"
- "Which pricing model will drive more annual contracts?"

Vague questions produce vague insights. Sharp questions produce actionable answers.

### Step 2: Choose Your Methodology

Match your method to your question:

| Question Type | Best Method |
|---------------|-------------|
| Why do customers behave this way? | Qualitative interviews |
| How many customers have this problem? | Survey or market data |
| Which message resonates better? | A/B test |
| What do customers wish we'd improve? | Open-ended survey + NPS |
| What does our competitive landscape look like? | Secondary research + win/loss interviews |

### Step 3: Collect Data

For **customer interviews**, the most valuable primary research tool:
1. Recruit 8-12 people who represent your target audience
2. Prepare an open-ended discussion guide, not a script
3. Ask about problems, not your product: "Tell me about the last time you struggled with X"
4. Listen more than you talk; follow interesting threads
5. Record (with permission) and take notes

**Key interview questions:**
- "What were you trying to accomplish when you [started using our product / faced this problem]?"
- "What did you try before finding us?"
- "What was frustrating about those alternatives?"
- "Walk me through your decision process."
- "What would you tell a friend about us?"

### Step 4: Analyze and Synthesize

Data without synthesis is noise. Look for:
- **Patterns** — themes that appear across multiple sources
- **Surprises** — things that contradict your assumptions
- **Language** — the exact words customers use to describe their problems (invaluable for copywriting)
- **Hierarchy** — not all findings are equally important; prioritize by frequency and impact

### Step 5: Generate Insights and Recommendations

An insight connects a finding to an implication. The format: "Because [finding], we should [recommendation]."

Example: "Because customers consistently describe their pain as 'drowning in notifications rather than lack of organization,' we should reframe our messaging around clarity and signal-to-noise reduction rather than productivity."

## Market Sizing: TAM, SAM, and SOM

Market research also informs market sizing — understanding the opportunity:

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** — the total revenue opportunity if you captured 100% of the market
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)** — the portion of TAM you can realistically serve given your current model
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** — the portion of SAM you can realistically capture in the near term

**Example for a B2B email marketing tool:**
- TAM: All businesses that use email marketing worldwide = $10B
- SAM: Small to mid-size e-commerce companies in English-speaking markets = $800M
- SOM: Realistic 3-year capture given resources and competition = $8M (1% of SAM)

## Budget-Friendly Research Methods

You don't need a research firm to get good insights:

1. **Customer interviews** — free except your time; 8-10 conversations is enough to find patterns
2. **Review mining** — read 100 Amazon/App Store/G2 reviews of competitors; tag themes
3. **Reddit research** — search relevant subreddits for discussions about your category
4. **Google Forms survey** — send to your email list; offer a small incentive
5. **Analytics audit** — what does Google Analytics/Mixpanel already tell you about behavior?
6. **"Mom test" conversations** — informal talks at industry events, coffee meetings, LinkedIn outreach

---

## Turning Research Into Strategy

Research has no value if it sits in a document nobody reads. Create a "Research Wall" — digital or physical — that organizes key findings into:

1. **Customer quotes** — verbatim language for messaging
2. **Problem hierarchy** — ranked list of problems by frequency and severity
3. **Decision drivers** — factors that influence purchase decisions
4. **Competitive perceptions** — how customers view alternatives
5. **Unmet needs** — gaps between what's available and what's desired

Review this wall before writing copy, planning campaigns, or building new features.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Market research validates assumptions and reduces the cost of wrong decisions
- Primary research (especially interviews) gives unique insights; secondary research provides context
- Define a specific research question before choosing a method
- The language customers use to describe their problems is marketing gold
- Research is only valuable when it leads to specific strategic recommendations

# Part 8: Positioning

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what positioning is and why it is the foundation of all marketing communications
- Learn the positioning process and frameworks used by professional marketers
- Create a positioning statement that guides consistent, differentiated marketing

---

## What Is Positioning?

Positioning is the act of defining how you want to be perceived in the mind of your target customer relative to alternatives.

The famous definition by Al Ries and Jack Trout in their landmark book *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981): "Positioning is not what you do to a product. It is what you do to the mind of the prospect."

This distinction is critical. Positioning doesn't live in your product — it lives in your customer's perception. You can't just declare a position; you must earn it through consistent communication and experience.

When someone thinks of project management software, they might think of Asana. When someone thinks of remote video calls, they think of Zoom. When someone thinks of electric vehicles, they think of Tesla. These brands have achieved strong positioning — they own a specific place in the customer's mental map.

---

## Why Positioning Matters

Without clear positioning:
- Your marketing messages are inconsistent and confusing
- Customers don't know why to choose you over alternatives
- Sales teams struggle to articulate your advantage
- You compete on price because there's no other reason to choose you
- Marketing spend is wasted reaching the wrong audiences with the wrong messages

With clear positioning:
- Every communication reinforces the same core perception
- Customers can easily articulate why they chose you (and refer others)
- You command premium pricing because you own a valuable position
- Marketing becomes more efficient because the message is consistent

## The Positioning Process

### Step 1: Know Your Target Customer

Positioning is always relative to a specific audience. The same product can be positioned differently for different segments. Start by confirming which customer you're positioning for (see [Part 2](./part-02-target-audience.md) and [Part 6](./part-06-buyer-personas.md)).

### Step 2: Define the Category

What category do you exist in? This is the frame of reference — the competitive context in which customers evaluate you.

Choosing your category is a strategic decision:
- **Positioning within an existing category:** Easier for customers to understand, but you're automatically compared to incumbents
- **Creating a new category:** Harder to explain initially, but you own it if successful

**Example:** When Salesforce launched in 1999, they could have positioned as "CRM software." Instead, they positioned as "software as a service" (SaaS) — a new category they largely invented. They won by owning a new frame of reference.

### Step 3: Identify Points of Parity and Difference

**Points of Parity (POPs):** Attributes you must match to be considered a credible competitor. Customers won't choose you if you're missing these, but having them doesn't make you win.

**Points of Difference (PODs):** Attributes where you're meaningfully better than competitors on dimensions customers care about. These are your basis for differentiation.

The goal is to match on parity and win on difference.

### Step 4: Identify the Competitive Advantage

Your POD must be:
- **Relevant** — does the target customer care about this?
- **Distinct** — do competitors not offer this equally well?
- **Believable** — can you credibly claim and prove this?

### Step 5: Write a Positioning Statement

The classic format (for internal use, not customer-facing):

> "For [target customer], [brand name] is the [category] that [key benefit/differentiation] because [reason to believe]."

**Example:** "For scaling tech startups, Notion is the all-in-one workspace that replaces fragmented tools because it combines docs, databases, and wikis in a flexible system anyone can customize."

## Positioning Frameworks

### Category + Differentiation
Position within a recognized category and differentiate on one key dimension.
- "The [superlative] [category] for [target customer]"
- Examples: "The easiest accounting software for freelancers" / "The fastest way to launch a landing page"

### Against the Leader
Position explicitly in comparison to the dominant player.
- Classic example: Avis "We're #2. We try harder."
- Risk: If the leader changes, your positioning changes with them

### Problem/Solution Positioning
Lead with the problem rather than the solution category.
- "Tired of [pain]? There's a better way."
- Works well for creating new categories or expanding beyond early adopters

### Value Positioning
Lead with the price/value equation.
- "Enterprise features at SMB prices"
- Risk: Can trigger a race to the bottom; use when price is genuinely disruptive

---

## Positioning Pitfalls

**Over-positioning:** Too narrow. You're so specialized that the market is too small.
**Under-positioning:** Too broad. No clear identity; you're whatever each customer needs you to be.
**Confused positioning:** Multiple different messages competing; customers can't form a coherent perception.
**Irrelevant positioning:** You've differentiated on something customers don't actually care about.
**Borrowed positioning:** You've claimed a position already owned by a competitor.

---

## Testing Your Positioning

Before finalizing, pressure-test your positioning:

1. **The blank mind test:** If a customer with no knowledge of your brand heard your positioning statement, would they have a clear, positive impression?
2. **The differentiation test:** Could a competitor say this with equal credibility? If yes, it's not differentiated.
3. **The relevance test:** Does your target customer's face light up when they hear it, or do they nod politely?
4. **The proof test:** Can you substantiate every claim you make?

---

## Positioning vs. Messaging

Positioning is internal strategy — the foundation. Messaging is how you express that positioning externally.

Your positioning statement becomes the source from which you derive:
- Website headlines
- Ad copy
- Elevator pitches
- Sales decks
- Content themes

Every external communication should be consistent with the positioning — even if the words change. Positioning is the melody; messaging is the lyrics.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Positioning is the perception you create in the customer's mind, not a feature of your product
- Strong positioning requires specificity: a clear category, a defined customer, a credible differentiation
- Points of parity must be matched; points of difference must be won
- The positioning statement is an internal tool; messaging translates it for external audiences
- Test your positioning for differentiation, relevance, and provability before committing

## What Is Synthetic Media?

Synthetic media is content — text, images, audio, or video — generated by artificial intelligence rather than recorded from the physical world or written by humans. The category includes:

- **AI-generated text** (large language models: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini)
- **AI-generated images** (diffusion models: DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion)
- **AI-generated audio** (voice synthesis: ElevenLabs, Resemble AI, Suno)
- **AI-generated video** (Sora, Runway, Pika, Synthesia)
- **Deepfakes** (AI-generated media featuring realistic likenesses of real people)

Synthetic media has existed since GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) became capable in the mid-2010s, but the quality, accessibility, and cost of generation improved dramatically from 2022 onward. Consumer-facing tools now enable high-quality synthetic media creation without technical expertise.

## Marketing Applications of Synthetic Media

**1. AI Video Production**
Synthesia enables brands to create talking-head video content with photorealistic AI avatars at a fraction of traditional production cost. Use cases: product demos, training videos, multilingual content localization (an AI avatar speaking your brand message in 50+ languages). Brands using Synthesia report 90%+ reduction in video production cost for applicable use cases.

**2. Personalized Video at Scale**
Combining AI video with dynamic personalization enables individually addressed video messages. A software company can send 10,000 prospects a personalized demo video featuring their name, company, and specific use case — at essentially zero marginal cost per video.

**3. AI Voice for Ads and Content**
Text-to-speech AI voice synthesis has reached near-human quality. Marketing teams use AI voice for podcast-style content, video narration, and audio ads without hiring voiceover talent. For localization, AI voice can synthesize content in dozens of languages while maintaining consistent brand voice characteristics.

**4. AI-Generated Visual Assets**
Brands use image generation AI (Midjourney, Adobe Firefly, DALL·E) to rapidly produce visual concepts, mood boards, campaign illustrations, and background assets. The workflow shifts from commissioning photography or illustration to prompting and curating.

**5. Dynamic Creative Optimization with AI**
Beyond static synthetic media, AI can dynamically assemble ad creatives — combining generated headlines, images, and CTAs in real time to create optimal combinations for specific audience segments and contexts.

## The Deepfake Problem

Deepfakes — AI-generated media featuring convincing likenesses of real people — represent the highest-risk dimension of synthetic media for marketing. Celebrity deepfakes in unauthorized advertisements have become a significant legal and reputational issue:

- Tom Hanks, Oprah Winfrey, and MrBeast have publicly warned audiences about unauthorized deepfake ads using their likenesses to promote scam products
- The FTC received thousands of deepfake ad complaints in 2023–2024
- Meta, YouTube, and TikTok have implemented policies requiring disclosure of AI-generated content containing realistic human likenesses

For legitimate marketers, the deepfake problem creates two challenges: legal risk from unauthorized likeness use and trust damage from operating in an ecosystem where consumers can no longer distinguish authentic from synthetic content.

---

## The Authenticity Trust Crisis

Research on consumer attitudes toward AI-generated content reveals a consistent pattern: when informed that content is AI-generated, consumers rate it as less trustworthy, less authentic, and less emotionally resonant — even when they cannot distinguish it from human-created content in blind tests (Jakesch et al., 2023; Epstein et al., 2023).

This creates a disclosure dilemma: disclosure is ethically required but commercially costly. Brands using AI content at scale face ongoing decisions about when, where, and how to disclose AI generation.

The European Union's AI Act (effective 2024–2026) mandates disclosure when AI-generated content could mislead audiences about its origin. Platform-level requirements (Meta, YouTube, TikTok) require disclosure for realistic AI-generated media in political advertising. Broader disclosure requirements are expanding.

---

## Research Gaps

**Gap 1: Long-term trust impact of AI content disclosure**
Short-term studies show trust penalties for disclosed AI content. But if AI content becomes ubiquitous and disclosure becomes standard practice, does the trust penalty diminish? Research on habituated consumer response to routine AI content disclosure is not yet available.

**Gap 2: Content performance: AI vs. human vs. hybrid**
Controlled studies comparing conversion performance, engagement, and brand perception metrics across AI-generated, human-created, and AI-assisted-human-reviewed content are sparse. Most comparisons are channel-specific and short-term.

**Gap 3: Deepfake detection and platform enforcement effectiveness**
Platform AI-detection systems are in an adversarial escalation with generation model improvements. The real-world effectiveness of deepfake detection and removal systems at protecting consumers and brands is inadequately studied.

**Gap 4: Psychological harm from AI-generated personalization**
If consumers receive highly personalized synthetic video messages at scale, what are the psychological effects? Does personalized synthetic outreach feel invasive or valued? Research on the emotional and behavioral response to AI-personalized video is nascent.

---

## Responsible Synthetic Media Guidelines

**For legitimate marketing use:**
1. Disclose AI generation when content could mislead about human involvement (spokesperson, testimonial, influencer)
2. Never use real people's likenesses without explicit consent
3. Maintain human editorial review for AI-generated customer-facing content
4. Test AI-generated vs. human content with your specific audience before scaling
5. Monitor evolving platform policies and regulatory requirements — this space is changing quarterly

**Brand voice protection:**
AI content generated without strong brand voice constraints will drift toward generic. Develop detailed style guides, example content sets, and prompt templates to maintain brand coherence in AI-assisted workflows.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Synthetic media spans AI-generated text, images, audio, and video — all with clear marketing applications across cost reduction, localization, and personalization
- The deepfake problem creates legal, trust, and platform policy risks that legitimate marketers must navigate carefully
- Consumer research shows trust penalties for disclosed AI content — disclosure is ethical but commercially costly
- Emerging regulations (EU AI Act, platform policies) are establishing disclosure requirements for realistic AI-generated media
- Research gaps: long-term trust habituation, comparative content performance, detection effectiveness, and psychological response to AI personalization

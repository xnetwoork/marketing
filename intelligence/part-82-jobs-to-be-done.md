# Part 82: Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Theory in Marketing Practice

> **Section 11: Marketing Intelligence & Decision Systems**

---

## Learning Objectives

- Apply Jobs-to-be-Done theory to positioning, messaging, and product marketing decisions
- Distinguish between functional, social, and emotional jobs across different customer contexts
- Identify the measurement gaps and practical limitations in JTBD research methodology

---

## The Core JTBD Insight

Jobs-to-be-Done theory, developed by Clayton Christensen and popularized by Tony Ulwick and Bob Moesta, proposes that customers don't buy products — they *hire* products to do a job. The job is the progress the customer is trying to make in a particular circumstance.

This reframing is deceptively powerful. It shifts the marketing question from "who is our customer?" to "what situation creates demand for what we sell?" The same demographic customer might hire a milkshake for two different jobs — a morning commute companion (where thickness and slow consumption are the key attributes) versus a children's treat (where speed of consumption and taste dominate). The jobs are completely different, even though the product is identical.

The marketing implication: when you understand the job, you understand the competitive set, the messaging that resonates, the channel where demand is triggered, and the switching conditions that make a customer leave.

---

## The Three Job Dimensions

**Functional Job**
The practical task the customer is trying to accomplish. "Get from location A to location B efficiently" is the functional job that Uber was hired to do — the same functional job that taxis, personal cars, and public transit all competed to fill.

**Social Job**
How the customer wants to be perceived by others as a result of using the product. Buying a Tesla isn't purely a functional transportation decision — the social job (signal environmental values, demonstrate early adopter identity, signal wealth) is inseparable from the purchase. Marketing that addresses only the functional job misses the decision architecture.

**Emotional Job**
How the customer wants to feel. Insurance products address the emotional job of feeling secure and protected. Premium audio equipment addresses the emotional job of feeling like a discerning audiophile. Emotional jobs often explain why customers pay premiums that can't be justified by functional attribute comparison.

---

## JTBD vs. Persona-Based Marketing

Traditional buyer personas describe *who* the customer is (demographic and psychographic profile). JTBD describes *why* they buy (the circumstance and progress they seek). These are complementary, not competing — but JTBD often reveals why persona-based campaigns underperform.

Personas group customers by attributes they share but may not have the same job. A 35-year-old marketing manager and a 35-year-old freelance consultant might have identical demographic profiles but hire project management software for completely different jobs — the former for team coordination, the latter for client reporting. Same persona, different job, different messaging.

JTBD grounds messaging in the customer's desired progress rather than their demographic profile. This is why JTBD-based messaging tends to feel more specific and resonant — it meets the customer in the situation that triggered demand.

---

## The Four Forces of Progress

Bob Moesta's "Four Forces" model maps the psychological dynamics that drive switching behavior:

**Push Forces** (drive customers away from the current solution):
1. **Push of the current situation** — frustrations, limitations, and anxieties about the status quo
2. **Anxiety about the new** — fear of change, uncertainty about whether the new solution will work

**Pull Forces** (attract customers toward the new solution):
3. **Pull of the new solution** — the appeal and promise of the new option
4. **Habit and inertia** — loyalty to familiar ways, even when they're suboptimal

Marketing implications: most campaigns only address Pull force #3 (the product's appeal). High-converting marketing also addresses Push force #1 (validating the customer's frustration), reduces Anxiety force #2 (risk reversal, social proof, free trials), and addresses Inertia force #4 (switching ease, migration support, testimonials from switchers).

## Applying JTBD to Marketing Decisions

**Positioning:** What job are you best hired to do? Position in the job space, not the feature space. "The CRM that actually gets used" (hired for the job of sales adoption, not sales capability) is JTBD positioning.

**Messaging:** Lead with the struggle/situation that triggers the job. "When your team can't find the file..." names the struggling moment before offering the solution. This is more powerful than "Organize your files with [Product]."

**Channel strategy:** Where do customers experience the struggling moment? That's where you want to be present. If the job is triggered during a specific workflow, product integrations and in-context advertising outperform top-of-funnel brand advertising.

**Competitive framing:** Who else is hired to do the same job? The real competitive set is often broader than the obvious category. Zoom doesn't just compete with WebEx — it competes with in-person meetings, phone calls, and email for the job of "align a distributed team quickly."

## Real-World Example: The Milkshake Study

Christensen's original milkshake research is the most cited JTBD case study. A fast food chain hired researchers to understand why customers bought milkshakes. Demographic analysis produced no actionable insights. But circumstance-based interviews revealed two distinct jobs: morning commute customers (who wanted something slow to consume during a long drive) and afternoon treat customers (who wanted a small indulgence for themselves or their children). Each job required completely different product attributes and marketing approaches — yet the product team had been optimizing for an undifferentiated "milkshake customer."

## Research Gaps

**Gap 1: JTBD interview reliability**
JTBD research depends heavily on retrospective customer interviews about switching decisions. Research on the reliability of retrospective recall in purchase contexts — how accurately customers can describe what actually drove their switching decisions — is limited. The gap between reported and actual motivations is a persistent challenge.

**Gap 2: Quantitative JTBD at scale**
Traditional JTBD research is qualitative (in-depth interviews). Scaling job identification to large customer bases — and statistically validating job hypotheses — requires quantitative methods that are less developed in the JTBD literature.

**Gap 3: JTBD in subscription and usage-based models**
JTBD was developed largely in context of discrete purchase decisions. In subscription businesses where value is demonstrated continuously, the "job hiring" metaphor is less clean. Research on how to apply JTBD frameworks to ongoing subscription engagement (rather than initial acquisition) is sparse.

---

## Key Takeaways

- JTBD asks "what job is the customer hiring this product to do?" rather than "who is the customer?"
- Jobs have three dimensions: functional (the task), social (the perception), and emotional (the feeling)
- The Four Forces model reveals why customers switch — addressing all four is more powerful than addressing only product appeal
- JTBD produces more specific, resonant messaging by meeting customers in the situation that triggered demand
- Research gaps: interview recall reliability, quantitative scaling methods, and application to subscription/usage models

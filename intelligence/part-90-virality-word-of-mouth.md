## Why Some Things Spread and Others Don't

Word-of-mouth has always been the most persuasive marketing channel — people trust recommendations from people they know far more than brand-originated messages. Nielsen consistently finds that 92% of consumers trust recommendations from friends and family more than any form of advertising. But word-of-mouth was historically undesignable — it happened (or didn't) based on product quality and luck.

Modern virality research — from Jonah Berger's STEPPS framework to BuzzSumo's large-scale content analysis to network science applied to information diffusion — has produced principles that make organic spread at least partially engineerable. Not predictably so — the complexity of social dynamics prevents precise virality prediction — but the probability of spread can be meaningfully increased through deliberate design choices.

---

## Jonah Berger's STEPPS Framework

Based on analysis of thousands of viral pieces of content and products, Jonah Berger's *Contagious* (2013) identified six factors that increase the probability of word-of-mouth transmission:

**Social Currency**
Things that make people look good, smart, exclusive, or in-the-know are more likely to be shared. Insider information, contrarian insights, early access signals, and exclusivity all confer social currency on the sharer. This is why "most people don't know this about [topic]" is such a durable content format — it lets the sharer give a gift of knowledge to their audience.

**Triggers**
Ideas that are frequently "triggered" by everyday cues spread further than ideas that are only activated by deliberate recall. "Like a Good Neighbor, State Farm Is There" — the jingle is triggered by any situation where you need a neighbor. KitKat's association with coffee breaks was deliberately engineered through a campaign linking coffee and KitKat, making the coffee trigger activate the KitKat thought.

**Emotion**
Content that generates high-arousal emotions (awe, excitement, anger, anxiety) spreads more than content that generates low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment). The valence matters less than the arousal level. This is why outrage content spreads faster than good news, and why awe-inspiring brand stories outperform "nice" brand stories.

**Public**
The more visible a behavior is, the more likely it is to be imitated. When product use is visible to non-users (the Apple laptop sticker, the AirPods visible in ears, the Glossier bag carried on the street), it functions as passive advertising that is more credible than brand-originated messages.

**Practical Value**
Useful content — tools, how-tos, frameworks, data — spreads because sharing useful things makes you helpful and therefore valuable to your social network. The "curse of knowledge" in content: experts forget that information obvious to them is not obvious to others. Information asymmetry creates sharing motivation.

**Stories**
People think in narratives, not arguments. Ideas embedded in compelling stories travel further than arguments or data because the story form is memorable and naturally repeatable. The best brand stories are "Trojan horses" — entertainment vehicles that carry the brand message while the audience is primarily engaged with the story.

## Network Science Applications

Social network research provides additional predictors of content spread:

**Weak tie advantage:** Mark Granovetter's "strength of weak ties" theory demonstrates that information spreads further through weak ties (acquaintances) than strong ties (close friends) because weak ties bridge different social clusters. Content that appeals to specific communities (strong tie clusters) but has general relevance spreads wider through weak tie bridges.

**Bridging vs. bonding:** Some people in networks are high-degree bridging nodes — they connect otherwise separate social clusters. Identifying and seeding high-bridging individuals (not necessarily the highest-follower influencers) is more effective for spreading ideas across diverse audiences.

**Cascade dynamics:** Information cascades — where early adoption signals trigger further adoption — are inherently unpredictable in magnitude but predictable in the conditions that enable them. Research shows that cascades require initial seeding of a small set of highly connected, high-trust nodes, followed by organic spread.

## Product Virality vs. Content Virality

Content virality (shared ideas) and product virality (new user acquisition through existing users) operate through partially different mechanisms:

Content virality: driven by STEPPS factors, emotional resonance, and social identity signaling
Product virality: driven by inherent/collaborative/expressive virality mechanics built into the product experience (see Part 83 on viral loops)

The most effective organic growth combines both: a product with built-in viral mechanics that generates shareable user stories.

## Research Gaps

**Gap 1: Virality prediction at scale**
Despite significant research, no reliable model can predict whether a specific piece of content will go viral before release. The sensitivity to initial conditions (which influential account shares first, what other events compete for attention, algorithm-specific dynamics) makes precise prediction impossible. Research on probabilistic virality estimation — "this content has a 15% probability of exceeding 100K shares" — rather than binary viral/not-viral prediction, is underdeveloped.

**Gap 2: Long-term brand effects of viral moments**
Viral content produces extraordinary awareness bursts. Research on whether viral moments translate into durable brand equity, sustained audience growth, or customer acquisition at proportional rates — versus generating temporary traffic spikes without lasting commercial effect — is sparse.

**Gap 3: Algorithm-mediated virality**
Social algorithms now mediate most content spread. Organic virality in 2026 is algorithm-dependent virality. Research on how platform algorithm changes affect the STEPPS factors' relative importance — and whether human psychological sharing motivations remain primary in algorithm-curated environments — is a significant open question.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Virality is engineerable but not predictable — design choices meaningfully increase spread probability without guaranteeing specific outcomes
- STEPPS: Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories — each independently increases sharing probability; combinations are multiplicative
- Network science adds: weak tie advantage, bridging node identification, and cascade condition design
- Product virality and content virality operate through related but distinct mechanisms — the most powerful growth combines both
- Research gaps: probabilistic virality prediction, long-term brand effects of viral moments, and algorithm-mediated virality dynamics

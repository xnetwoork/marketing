# Part 47: Performance Reporting

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the purpose and principles of effective marketing performance reporting
- Learn how to structure reports for different audiences and purposes
- Build reporting habits that drive accountability and continuous improvement

---

## The Purpose of Reporting

A marketing report is not a data dump. It is a structured communication designed to:
1. Show what happened and why
2. Evaluate performance against goals
3. Identify what to do differently
4. Build trust and credibility with stakeholders

Reports that don't accomplish these goals are theater — they consume time without producing value. The measure of a good report is whether it leads to a better decision.

---

## Reporting Cadences

Different stakeholders need different information at different frequencies:

### Daily Monitoring (Not a Report — An Alert System)
Who: Channel managers, marketing ops
Format: Automated alerts when key metrics deviate significantly from expected values
Purpose: Catch problems early (campaign budget exhausted, tracking code broken, conversion rate anomaly)
Tool: Google Analytics alerts, ad platform notifications

### Weekly Report
Who: Marketing team and immediate manager
Format: Concise (1-2 pages or slides); focus on what happened this week and what's happening next week
Key content:
- Progress on key metrics vs. weekly goals
- Highlights and wins
- Issues and blockers
- Planned actions

Purpose: Team alignment and course correction

### Monthly Report
Who: Marketing leadership and cross-functional stakeholders
Format: 3-5 pages or a dashboard view
Key content:
- Performance vs. monthly goals (each key KPI)
- Channel performance summary
- Notable campaigns: results vs. objectives
- Key insights and learnings
- Planned optimizations
- Next month priorities

Purpose: Strategic course correction; stakeholder trust building

### Quarterly Business Review (QBR)
Who: Senior leadership (VP, CMO, CEO, board)
Format: Presentation (10-15 slides) + appendix data
Key content:
- Q performance vs. quarterly goals
- Attribution analysis: marketing's contribution to pipeline and revenue
- Channel ROI analysis
- Strategic insights: what did we learn?
- Next quarter strategy and focus
Purpose: Strategic alignment; budget justification; leadership confidence

## The Report Structure

### The Executive Summary
Start with the bottom line. Stakeholders should understand the key message in the first 30 seconds:
- Are we on track or off track?
- What's the most important thing that happened?
- What's the most important thing we're changing?

### Performance vs. Goals
For each key KPI:
- Goal for the period
- Actual result
- Variance (% above/below goal)
- Trend (better or worse than last period?)

Use simple visual indicators: green checkmark (on track), yellow caution (slightly off), red (significantly off).

### Analysis and Insights
Don't just report numbers — explain them:
- Why did a metric improve or decline?
- What external factors influenced results (seasonality, competitive moves, market events)?
- What internal changes affected results?

**The format:** "X happened because Y. This means Z."

### Actions and Recommendations
Every report should conclude with clear next actions:
- What are we changing based on this month's data?
- What tests are we running?
- Where are we reallocating budget?

A report without recommended actions is an information exercise, not a decision-making tool.

## Data Visualization Best Practices

Visual communication is faster and more persuasive than tables of numbers.

**Use the right chart type:**
- **Line chart:** Trends over time
- **Bar chart:** Comparisons across categories
- **Pie/donut:** Composition (use sparingly; hard to read small differences)
- **Funnel:** Sequential conversion rates
- **Scatter plot:** Correlations between two variables

**Chart best practices:**
- Use titles that state the insight, not just the metric: "Organic traffic up 23% YoY" not "Organic Traffic"
- Keep color usage minimal; use contrasting color for the key data point
- Add reference lines for goals and benchmarks
- Remove grid lines and chart junk that distract from the message

**The IBCS (International Business Communication Standards)** provides excellent guidelines for business data visualization if you want to go deep.

## Building Reporting Discipline

Common reporting failures and how to prevent them:

**"We don't have time to report"**
Build reporting into the workflow, not as an addition. Use dashboards to automate data collection; standardize report templates to reduce production time.

**"Reports take a week to pull together"**
Invest in data infrastructure upfront. If pulling data takes a week every month, that's a sign to invest in better analytics tooling or cleaner data pipelines.

**"Nobody reads the reports"**
Ask whether reports are answering the questions stakeholders actually have. Reports that don't answer relevant questions get ignored. Interview stakeholders: "What would make this report more useful?"

**"The data doesn't add up across platforms"**
Establish an authoritative source of truth for each metric and stick to it. When platforms disagree, define which one takes precedence and why.

## Key Reporting Templates

### Monthly Marketing Report Template

**Section 1: Executive Summary** (5 bullet points)
**Section 2: KPI Scorecard** (Table: metric, goal, actual, variance, trend)
**Section 3: Channel Performance** (1 paragraph + chart per channel)
**Section 4: Campaign Results** (Table: campaign, objective, spend, results, notes)
**Section 5: Insights and Learnings** (Bulleted; what we learned from this month)
**Section 6: Next Month Plan** (Priorities, changes based on learnings, any budget shifts)

---

## Key Takeaways

- Effective reports answer: What happened? Why? What should we do differently?
- Different cadences serve different audiences: daily alerts for operators, weekly for teams, monthly for leadership, quarterly for strategy
- Lead with the executive summary; stakeholders should understand the key message in 30 seconds
- Every report should end with specific actions and recommendations
- Invest in data infrastructure and report templates to make reporting efficient and consistent

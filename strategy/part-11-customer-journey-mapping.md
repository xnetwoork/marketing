# Part 11: Customer Journey Mapping

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the customer journey as a strategic tool, not just a visualization
- Learn how to build a journey map that reveals real opportunities for improvement
- Align marketing, sales, and customer success around the complete customer experience

---

## What Is the Customer Journey?

The customer journey is the complete sequence of experiences a customer has with your brand — from the moment they first become aware of you to their ongoing relationship as a loyal customer (or their departure as a churned one).

It is not a funnel (though funnels are a simplified representation of part of it). A funnel implies a linear path with clear stages. The real customer journey is messy, non-linear, and influenced by dozens of touchpoints across multiple channels.

**Why it matters:** Customers don't experience your "marketing department," your "sales team," and your "customer success team" separately. They experience your company. A customer journey map forces you to see your business the way customers actually experience it — and that perspective reveals problems and opportunities that siloed thinking misses.

---

## The Five Stages of the Customer Journey

While journeys vary by business model and category, most share five broad stages:

### 1. Awareness
The customer realizes they have a problem or desire, or they first encounter your brand.

- **What triggers this stage?** A painful event, a recommendation from a colleague, an ad, a piece of content, a search query
- **What the customer is thinking:** "I have this problem... is there a solution?"
- **Marketing's job:** Get found, create interest, frame the problem in terms that resonate

### 2. Consideration
The customer is actively evaluating solutions, including yours.

- **What the customer is doing:** Researching options, reading reviews, watching demos, comparing features and pricing
- **What they need:** Credible information, social proof, differentiation clarity
- **Marketing's job:** Provide the right content at the right time; make it easy to evaluate you favorably

### 3. Decision
The customer selects a solution and makes a purchase (or decides not to buy at all).

- **What influences this stage:** Final pricing clarity, proof of ROI, ease of getting started, sales conversation quality, competitive comparison
- **Marketing's job:** Remove friction, reinforce the decision, provide confidence

### 4. Onboarding & First Value
The customer begins using your product and either experiences early success or early frustration.

- **The critical insight:** In SaaS and subscription models, the sale is not the finish line — it's the starting line. Customers who don't achieve early value churn.
- **Marketing's job:** Welcome, activate, guide to first value moment quickly

### 5. Retention & Advocacy
Long-term customers who renew, expand, and refer others.

- **What drives this stage:** Ongoing value delivery, relationship quality, recognition, exclusive benefits
- **Marketing's job:** Deepen engagement, reward loyalty, create advocates and referrers

## How to Build a Customer Journey Map

### Step 1: Choose Your Scope

Are you mapping the journey for a specific:
- **Customer persona?** (Different personas have different journeys)
- **Product or service?**
- **Purchase type?** (New acquisition vs. renewal vs. upsell)

Start with one specific scenario. Mapping "the customer journey" in general produces a useless average.

### Step 2: Define the Stages

Adapt the standard stages to your business context. B2B software might have: Aware → Evaluate → Trial → Purchase → Onboard → Expand. B2C retail might have: Discover → Research → Visit → Purchase → Use → Return.

### Step 3: Identify Touchpoints

For each stage, list every point of contact between the customer and your brand:

- Website pages visited
- Emails received and opened
- Ads seen
- Social content encountered
- Sales conversations
- Support interactions
- Product use moments
- Invoices and billing communications

Most companies discover they have far more touchpoints than they realized — and that many of them are inconsistent or broken.

### Step 4: Map Customer Experience

For each stage and touchpoint, document:
- **What is the customer doing?** (Behavioral)
- **What is the customer thinking?** (Cognitive)
- **What is the customer feeling?** (Emotional)
- **What questions do they have?**
- **What frustrations or pain points arise?**

Use real customer research — interviews, support tickets, session recordings — not assumptions.

### Step 5: Assess Your Performance

For each touchpoint, evaluate:
- How well are we serving the customer's needs at this moment?
- Are there gaps, friction points, or broken experiences?
- Where are customers dropping off or getting stuck?

Use quantitative data (drop-off rates, time spent, click rates) to validate qualitative insights.

### Step 6: Identify Opportunities

Map improvements: which touchpoints, if improved, would have the highest impact on the customer experience and on your business outcomes?

Prioritize by: impact × feasibility ÷ cost.

---

## The Moments of Truth

Not all touchpoints are equal. Certain moments disproportionately shape the customer's overall perception:

- **First moment of truth** (Procter & Gamble concept): The moment a customer first encounters your product on the "shelf" (physical or digital)
- **Second moment of truth:** The experience of using the product for the first time
- **Zero moment of truth** (Google concept): The research moment — what a customer finds when they Google you before the first moment of truth

Mapping helps you identify your most critical moments of truth and ensure they're exceptional.

---

## Cross-Functional Journey Mapping

Customer journey maps are most powerful when built and used cross-functionally:
- **Marketing** owns the awareness and consideration stages
- **Sales** owns the decision stage
- **Product/Customer Success** owns onboarding and retention
- **Support** handles moments of distress throughout

When all functions use the same journey map, the handoffs between stages improve dramatically — and customers feel a more consistent, cohesive experience.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The customer journey encompasses all experiences with your brand, not just the marketing funnel
- Journeys are non-linear and multi-touchpoint; map real behavior, not assumed paths
- Use research (interviews, analytics, session recordings) to populate journey maps with truth
- Identify and prioritize improvement opportunities based on customer impact and business outcomes
- Cross-functional alignment on the journey improves handoffs and overall customer experience

# Part 13: Go-to-Market Strategy

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the purpose and components of a go-to-market (GTM) strategy
- Learn how to build a GTM plan for a new product, market, or initiative
- Avoid the most common GTM execution failures

---

## What Is a Go-to-Market Strategy?

A go-to-market (GTM) strategy is an action plan that specifies how a company will reach target customers and achieve competitive advantage when launching a product, entering a new market, or scaling an existing business.

Think of it as the bridge between product development and commercial success. A great product without a GTM strategy rarely reaches its full potential. Conversely, even an imperfect product can succeed with a brilliant GTM approach.

The GTM strategy answers:
- **Who** is the ideal customer?
- **What** is the value proposition for each segment?
- **Where** will customers be reached?
- **How** will we price and package?
- **Why** will customers choose us over alternatives?
- **When** will key milestones occur?

---

## GTM vs. Marketing Strategy

These terms are often used interchangeably, but there's a useful distinction:

- **Marketing strategy** is ongoing — it guides how you communicate and create demand over time
- **GTM strategy** is often event-based — it focuses specifically on the launch or entry into a market

In practice, a GTM strategy contains a marketing strategy as one of its components.

## The Core Components of a GTM Strategy

### 1. Market Definition
Clearly define the market you're entering:
- **TAM/SAM/SOM** sizing (see [Part 3](../foundations/part-03-market-research.md))
- Primary and secondary target segments
- Geographic focus
- Competitive landscape summary

### 2. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)
Your most valuable, best-fit customers — the ones you'll build around. For B2B, the ICP includes:
- Industry and company size
- Technology environment
- Business situation (growth stage, pain trigger)
- Decision-maker profile

For B2C, the ICP maps to your highest-value buyer personas.

### 3. Value Proposition and Messaging
What you'll communicate to each segment, in their language, addressing their specific pain points and desired outcomes (see [Part 5](../foundations/part-05-value-proposition.md) and [Part 21](../content/part-21-messaging-framework.md)).

### 4. Positioning
How you'll be perceived relative to alternatives in each target segment (see [Part 8](../foundations/part-08-positioning.md)).

### 5. Pricing and Packaging
How you'll monetize the product:
- Pricing model (subscription, one-time, usage-based, freemium)
- Package tiers and what's included
- Entry-point pricing and expansion path

### 6. Sales and Distribution Model
How customers will buy:
- **Sales-led:** Human sales team drives acquisition
- **Marketing-led:** Content and campaigns drive inbound leads that convert with lighter sales involvement
- **Product-led:** The product itself is the primary acquisition and expansion vehicle (free tier, viral loops, in-product upgrades)
- **Partner-led:** Resellers, agencies, or technology partners drive distribution

### 7. Customer Acquisition Strategy
Which channels and tactics you'll use to acquire customers, in what priority, with what budget and expected returns.

### 8. Launch Plan
The specific sequence of activities for launching:
- Pre-launch: Build awareness, create anticipation, recruit early adopters
- Launch: Make the announcement, drive initial conversions
- Post-launch: Capture momentum, iterate based on feedback

## GTM Motion: Sales-Led vs. Product-Led

One of the most important GTM decisions is the primary motion:

**Sales-Led Growth (SLG):**
- Sales team drives acquisition through outbound prospecting, demos, and relationship selling
- Higher-touch, higher-cost, appropriate for complex, high-ticket products
- Marketing role: generate qualified leads for sales to convert
- Best for: Enterprise B2B, complex solutions, high ACVs

**Product-Led Growth (PLG):**
- The product drives its own acquisition through free tier, viral features, or self-service conversion
- Lower-cost, scalable, requires product that delivers value quickly
- Marketing role: drive signups, support activation, convert free to paid
- Best for: Developer tools, productivity software, SMB SaaS

**Community-Led Growth:**
- A community around shared interest, profession, or identity drives awareness and credibility
- Marketing role: build and nurture community, convert community engagement to customers
- Best for: Tools for communities that already exist (developers, designers, creators)

## GTM Launch Framework: Pre/During/Post

### Pre-Launch (4-8 weeks before)
- Finalize positioning and messaging
- Build landing pages, sales materials, and onboarding flows
- Seed early content (SEO-targeted, thought leadership)
- Build the email list and beta user community
- Brief press contacts and analysts
- Train sales team on new positioning

### Launch Week
- Announce on all owned channels (email list, social, website)
- Execute PR outreach
- Activate paid campaigns
- Reach out directly to high-potential prospects
- Engage community (Product Hunt, Hacker News, relevant forums)

### Post-Launch (4-12 weeks after)
- Capture customer testimonials and early case studies
- Analyze what worked and didn't in acquisition
- Optimize onboarding based on early user behavior
- Iterate messaging based on sales conversations and customer feedback
- Double down on highest-performing channels

## Common GTM Failures

1. **Skipping the ICP** — launching for "everyone" with no focused first wave
2. **Misaligned pricing** — price doesn't match the segment's willingness to pay or perceived value
3. **Wrong motion** — using sales-led approach for a PLG product (or vice versa)
4. **No activation plan** — focusing on acquisition without ensuring customers get value
5. **One-and-done launch** — treating launch as an event rather than an ongoing process
6. **Ignoring the competition** — launching without addressing why customers should switch

---

## Key Takeaways

- GTM strategy bridges product development and commercial success by defining who, what, where, how, and when
- The choice of GTM motion (sales-led, product-led, community-led) shapes everything from team structure to marketing tactics
- A successful launch requires pre-launch preparation, launch execution, and post-launch iteration
- The ICP is the foundation of GTM — without a focused first customer, GTM energy is diffused
- Treat launch as the beginning of customer learning, not the end of product development

# Part 15: Pricing Strategies

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the strategic dimensions of pricing and its marketing implications
- Learn the major pricing models and when each applies
- Use pricing as a competitive and positioning lever, not just a financial calculation

---

## Pricing Is Marketing

Most companies treat pricing as a finance function: calculate costs, add a margin, set a price. This is a mistake. Pricing is one of the most powerful marketing levers available.

Price communicates:
- **Quality and positioning** — premium price signals premium product
- **Target segment** — price filters which customers you attract
- **Competitive stance** — pricing above, at, or below competitors says something about how you compete
- **Business model** — subscription vs. one-time vs. usage-based affects customer relationships and growth patterns

Getting pricing right can double revenue without changing anything else. Getting it wrong can undermine your positioning, attract the wrong customers, and create a ceiling on growth.

---

## The Three Pricing Foundations

Every pricing decision rests on three inputs:

### 1. Cost (Floor)
Your costs establish the minimum viable price — the floor below which you lose money. Understand all relevant costs:
- **Variable costs:** Direct costs per unit or customer (production, delivery, support)
- **Fixed costs:** Overhead that must be spread across customers (product development, infrastructure)
- **Customer acquisition cost (CAC):** Often overlooked; price must support profitable acquisition

### 2. Value (Ceiling)
The maximum a customer is willing to pay is set by the value they perceive. Value-based pricing aligns price with customer outcomes:
- If your product saves a customer $50,000/year, they might pay $10,000/year (5:1 ROI)
- If your product generates $500,000 in additional revenue, pricing at $50,000 is defensible

Quantify the value your product delivers. Customer interviews and case studies reveal this.

### 3. Competition (Reference)
Competitors set customer expectations. Customers use competitor pricing as a reference point when evaluating your price. You can price above (justify with differentiation), at (compete on other factors), or below (compete on cost).

## Major Pricing Strategies

### Cost-Plus Pricing
Add a fixed markup to cost. Simple, predictable, but ignores market reality.
- **Use when:** Simple commodity products where margin expectations are clear
- **Avoid when:** You're building a differentiated product with premium positioning

### Value-Based Pricing
Price based on what customers will pay for the value received.
- **Use when:** You can quantify the value delivered and differentiate clearly
- **Advantage:** Captures more of the value you create; signals premium quality
- **Challenge:** Requires deep customer understanding and willingness to test

### Competitive Pricing
Set prices in relation to competitor prices.
- **Use when:** Your product is similar to alternatives and customers easily compare
- **Advantage:** Lowers cognitive load for price-sensitive buyers
- **Risk:** Triggers price wars; commoditizes your offering

### Penetration Pricing
Enter the market at a low price to gain market share quickly, then raise prices over time.
- **Use when:** Building a new market or needing scale to achieve cost advantages
- **Classic example:** Netflix entering streaming far below pay-TV prices
- **Risk:** Attracts price-sensitive customers who may churn when prices rise

### Premium (Skimming) Pricing
Launch at a high price to capture early-adopter surplus, gradually lowering price as market matures.
- **Use when:** Product is genuinely innovative; early adopters have high willingness to pay
- **Classic example:** New iPhone models launch at premium; older models stay at lower prices
- **Risk:** Limits initial addressable market; invites competitive entry

### Freemium Pricing
Offer a free tier to drive adoption; monetize through upgrades to paid plans.
- **Use when:** The product has a wide potential user base and network effects; the free tier creates genuine value while limiting key features
- **Classic examples:** Spotify, Slack, Dropbox, Zoom
- **Challenge:** Freemium requires very efficient paid conversion; free users create costs without revenue

### Usage-Based (Consumption) Pricing
Customers pay for what they use — per API call, per GB stored, per transaction.
- **Use when:** Value scales with usage; customers have variable needs
- **Examples:** AWS, Twilio, Stripe
- **Advantage:** Lowers entry barrier; revenue scales with customer success
- **Challenge:** Unpredictable revenue; customers may over-optimize usage

## Pricing Architecture: Good/Better/Best

Most B2B and subscription products benefit from a tiered pricing structure:

| Tier | Target | Price | Purpose |
|------|--------|-------|---------|
| **Starter/Basic** | Small customers, individuals | Low | Drive volume; reduce entry barrier |
| **Professional/Growth** | Core target segment | Mid (anchor) | Main revenue; best ROI |
| **Enterprise/Custom** | Large organizations | High/Custom | Maximum value extraction; relationship |

The middle tier is the **anchor** — it's what most customers should and will choose. The high tier makes the middle look reasonable (decoy effect). The low tier reduces friction to entry.

## The Psychology of Pricing

Behavioral economics reveals that perceived price is as important as actual price:

- **Charm pricing:** $9.99 is perceived as significantly less than $10 despite the 1-cent difference
- **Anchoring:** The first price seen sets reference for all subsequent judgments (show annual price to make monthly look small)
- **Decoy effect:** A third, less attractive option makes the preferred option seem better by comparison
- **Framing:** "$10/day" feels less than "$3,650/year" even when identical
- **Loss aversion:** "Don't lose $5,000/year to inefficiency" is often more motivating than "Save $5,000/year"

---

## Pricing Testing

Price is one of the highest-impact variables you can test:
1. **Survey-based research:** Conjoint analysis and Van Westendorp price sensitivity surveys
2. **A/B testing:** Show different prices to different website visitors (ethically, transparently)
3. **Cohort analysis:** Compare LTV and retention across customers acquired at different price points

**Warning:** Be transparent with customers and avoid egregious price discrimination that damages trust.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Pricing is marketing — it signals quality, attracts segments, and shapes competitive position
- Build pricing from three inputs: cost (floor), value (ceiling), and competition (reference)
- Match your pricing model to your product and customer dynamics
- Tiered good/better/best architecture works for most subscription products
- Test pricing continuously; pricing intuition is often wrong, and data reveals better strategies
